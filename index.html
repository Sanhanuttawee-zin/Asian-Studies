006 â€” Local Entertainment

Art, Music & Cinema

Despite hardships, Afghan creativity has never been extinguished â€” from ancient poetry to contemporary music and film.

Music: The Rubab & Beyond

The rubab (a short-necked lute) is Afghanistan's national instrument â€” called the "Lion of Instruments." Traditional music forms include Ghazal poetry-song and lively Attan dance rhythms.

Afghan pop music flourished in the 1970sâ€“80s; artists like Ahmad Zahir (dubbed "Afghanistan's Elvis") remain deeply beloved. Today a new generation of artists from the diaspora carry this tradition forward globally.

Afghan cinema produced memorable films before the 1990s. Internationally, director Mohsen Makhmalbaf and documentarians have brought Afghan stories to the world stage.



Attan is Afghanistan's national dance, performed in a circle with synchronized spinning movements. It's performed at weddings, festivals, and gatherings â€” originally a warrior's pre-battle ritual for Pashtun tribes, now enjoyed across all ethnicities.

Kite fighting is a beloved Afghan pastime, made globally famous by Khaled Hosseini's novel The Kite Runner. Competitors coat strings in glass powder and try to cut rivals' kite lines. In Kabul's hills, kite season transforms the skyline into a colorful battlefield of silk and string.

Afghanistan has one of the world's richest oral traditions. The 13th-century Persian-language poet Rumi was born in Balkh (modern-day Afghanistan). Poetry competitions (mushairas) and storytelling evenings remain central to social life. Even illiterate Afghans memorize hundreds of poetic couplets.
